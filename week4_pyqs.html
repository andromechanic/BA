01

Application Strategy & Common Pitfalls

When solving applied elasticity problems, the most critical step is identifying which elasticity framework to deploy based on the given variables. Using the wrong definition for a specific problem type is the most common mathematical pitfall.

Point Elasticity (Derivative)

Used when you are given an explicit equation (e.g., $D = 1500 - 20P$) and asked for elasticity at a specific single price point.

Arc Elasticity (Percentage)

Used when you are given two discrete states (an initial Price/Quantity and a final Price/Quantity) without an underlying equation.

Crucial Wordplay Warning

Pay extreme attention to phrasing like "raised BY ₹5" versus "raised TO ₹5". The former indicates a delta ($\Delta P$), while the latter indicates the final absolute value ($P_f$). Failing to make this distinction breaks all percentage change formulas.

Linear Demand Model (Solved)

Given Equation

$$ D(P) = 1500 - 20P $$
Question 1

Market Size ($D_0$)

The absolute maximum demand when product is given for free ($P = 0$).

1500 units

Question 2

Satiating Price ($P_{sat}$)

The exact price where demand drops to zero. $1500 - 20P = 0$

₹ 75

Question 3

Elasticity at $P=50$

Using $E = \left|\frac{mP}{D_0 - mP}\right|$.
$(20 \cdot 50) / (1500 - 1000)$

$E = 2$

Because $E = 2$ (which is $> 1$), the product is highly elastic at $P=50$. A 10% change in price causes a 20% opposite change in demand.

03

Discrete Data & Percentage Change

The Buttermilk Scenario

A grocer raises the price of buttermilk by ₹5 to ₹15. The demand falls from 100 units to 80 units. Calculate the elasticity and state the product's nature.

Initial Price ($P_i$)

₹ 10

Final Price ($P_f$)

₹ 15

Initial Qty ($Q_i$)

100

Final Qty ($Q_f$)

80

Apply the base percentage change formula:

$$ E = \left| \frac{(80 - 100) / 100}{(15 - 10) / 10} \right| = \left| \frac{-20\%}{+50\%} \right| = 0.4 $$
💡

Conclusion: Inelastic ($E < 1$)

Because the 50% price hike only caused a 20% drop in volume, the product is inelastic. The grocer successfully increased overall revenue by raising the price.

04

Non-Linear Model Extraction

Given Two Observations on a Constant Curve

If a dataset explicitly states it follows a constant elasticity curve ($D = C \cdot P^{-\epsilon}$), you can extract parameters perfectly using just two coordinates.

$P_1 = 20, \ D_1 = 0.5$
$P_2 = 40, \ D_2 = 0.25$
1

Extract Elasticity ($\epsilon$)

Use the logarithmic ratio derived from dividing the two state equations: $$ \epsilon = \frac{\ln(D_1/D_2)}{\ln(P_2/P_1)} = \frac{\ln(0.5/0.25)}{\ln(40/20)} = \frac{\ln(2)}{\ln(2)} = 1 $$

2

Calculate Constant ($C$)

Plug the elasticity back into either coordinate pair to solve for the scaling constant $C$: $$ C = D_1 \cdot P_1^\epsilon = 0.5 \cdot 20^1 = 10 $$

Final Equation: $D(P) = 10 \cdot P^{-1}$

Theoretical Myth Busted

Satiating Price on a Constant Curve?

It does not exist. For $D = C \cdot P^{-\epsilon}$, there is no mathematical value for $P$ that makes $D = 0$. The curve approaches the axis asymptotically but never touches it.

Regression Evaluation (ANOVA)

When modeling demand via simple linear regression in software (like Excel), understanding the underlying relationships in the ANOVA output table is vital for interpreting the statistical significance of your fit.

Null Hypotheses ($H_0$)

  • • Intercept is not significant: $\beta_0 = 0$
  • • Slope is not significant: $\beta_1 = 0$

We want to reject these to prove our model captures a real trend.

Degrees of Freedom ($df$) Rules

  • Total $df$ $n - 1$
  • Regression $df$ $k \ (\text{variables})$
  • Residual $df$ $n - k - 1$

Observation Count ($n$) = Total $df$ + 1

Reconstructing the Output

Source Sum of Squares (SS) Mean Square (MS) F-Statistic
Regression SS_{Reg} $\frac{SS_{Reg}}{df_{Reg}}$ $\frac{MS_{Reg}}{MS_{Res}}$
Residual (Error) SS_{Res} $\frac{SS_{Res}}{df_{Res}}$
Total $SS_{Reg} + SS_{Res}$
