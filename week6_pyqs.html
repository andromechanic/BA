01

Non-Linear Predictions (Log-Log)

Scenario: GDP (%) and Urban Population (Crores) show a non-linear relationship. A natural logarithm ($ln$) transformation is applied to both to fit a Simple Linear Regression model.

The Transformed Equation

$$ \ln(UP) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \ln(GDP) $$

Q: How many degrees of freedom in Regression?
A: 1. Because only $1$ independent variable ($\ln(GDP)$) is used to predict the output.

Model Coefficients

  • Intercept ($\beta_0$) -24.42
  • Slope ($\beta_1$) 10.43

Note: In software output, the Intercept might be labeled as "_constant". Do not blindly assume the first row is the slope.

Solved Prediction Calculation

Problem: Predict the Urban Population ($UP$) in Crores when GDP is 15%.

1. Take log of input: $\ln(15) \approx 2.708$
2. Apply formula: $\ln(UP) = -24.42 + 10.43(2.708) = 3.82$
3. Convert back (Inverse Log): $UP = e^{3.82} \approx 45.60 \text{ Crores}$
02

Reverse-Engineering the ANOVA Table

Scenario (Milo Bakery): Data collected over 5 days for 3 products (Biscuits, Cake, Drink) to predict gas consumed. Parts of the ANOVA table are missing.

1

Deducing Degrees of Freedom

From "5 days" ($n=5$) and "3 products" ($k=3$):

Total: $n-1 = 4$
Reg: $k = 3$
Res: $5-3-1 = 1$
2

Finding Mean Square & Sum of Squares Residual

Given Standard Error ($SE = 3.36$):

  • $MS_{Res} = SE^2 = 3.36^2 \approx 11.2896$
  • $SS_{Res} = MS_{Res} \times df_{Res} = 11.2896 \times 1 = 11.2896$
3

Using Adjusted R² to find Total Sum of Squares

Given Adjusted $R^2 = 0.97$. Use the formula to back-solve for $R^2$, then $SS_{Tot}$:

$$ 0.97 = 1 - \left[ \frac{(1 - R^2)(4)}{1} \right] $$

$$ (1 - R^2) \times 4 = 0.03 \implies 1 - R^2 = 0.0075 $$

Since $1 - R^2 = \frac{SS_{Res}}{SS_{Tot}}$, then $SS_{Tot} = \frac{11.2896}{0.0075} \approx 1505.28$

03

Basic ANOVA Formulations

Scenario: A model with equation $Y = 4.1 - \beta_1 X_1 - \beta_2 X_2 - \beta_3 X_3$ is fit on 50 data points. Given $SS_{Res} = 900$ and $SS_{Tot} = 1400$.

Degrees of Freedom Extraction

  • ▶ $n = 50, \ k = 3$
  • ▶ $df_{Tot} = 50 - 1 = 49$
  • ▶ $df_{Reg} = k = 3$
  • ▶ $df_{Res} = 50 - 3 - 1 = 46$

F-Statistic Calculation

  • 1. $SS_{Reg} = 1400 - 900 = 500$
  • 2. $MS_{Reg} = 500 / 3 \approx 166.67$
  • 3. $MS_{Res} = 900 / 46 \approx 19.56$
  • $F = \frac{MS_{Reg}}{MS_{Res}} \approx 8.5185$

Adjusted R² Calculation (Percentage Trap)

$$ R^2 = \frac{SS_{Reg}}{SS_{Tot}} = \frac{500}{1400} \approx 0.3571 $$ $$ R^2_{adj} = 1 - (1 - 0.3571) \times \frac{49}{46} \approx 0.3151 $$
⚠️ Exam Trap: "Enter answer in percentage" → Output must be exactly 31.51.
04

Path Diagrams & Indirect Effects

Scenario (Milo's Motor): Explanatory variables are Mileage ($X_1$), Storage ($X_2$), and Charging Time ($X_3$). Dependent variable is Sales Volume ($Y$). 10 different regression models are provided.
Goal: Calculate the Total Indirect Effect of Mileage ($X_1$) on Sales ($Y$).

Path Formula

$$ \text{Indirect Effect} = (X_1 \xrightarrow{D} X_2 \xrightarrow{B} Y) + (X_1 \xrightarrow{E} X_3 \xrightarrow{C} Y) $$
Crucial Trick

The Model Selection Trick

You cannot pull coefficients randomly. You must extract them from the correct models representing the exact partial relationships:

  • Finding D ($X_1 \rightarrow X_2$): Look for the simple model predicting Storage ($X_2$) purely from Mileage ($X_1$).
    From Model 6: Coefficient = 0.9388
  • Finding E ($X_1 \rightarrow X_3$): Look for the simple model predicting Charging Time ($X_3$) purely from Mileage ($X_1$).
    From Model 4: Coefficient = -2.3
  • Finding B & C ($X \rightarrow Y$): ⚠️ You MUST use the FULL model (Model 10) that includes $X_1, X_2, \text{ and } X_3$ simultaneously. Why? Because you need the partial slopes holding the other variables constant.
    From Model 10: Storage coeff ($B$) = 8.9 | Charging coeff ($C$) = 19.40
Calculation: $(0.9388 \times 8.9) + (-2.3 \times 19.40) \approx -36.26$
05

Definitions & Statistical Traps

📚

Definition: Multiple R

The explicit correlation coefficient between the actual values of the dependent variable ($Y$) and the predicted values ($\hat{Y}$).

🔗

Definition: Multicollinearity

Refers strictly to a high correlation existing between two or more independent variables ($X_1, X_2 \dots$). It has nothing to do with the dependent variable.

🛑

Hypothesis Conclusion Trap

If asked "What do you conclude about Dr. Milo?" and all variable p-values are $> \alpha$ (e.g., $> 0.15$):

Answer: We fail to reject $H_0$. "Dr. Milo is dumb" (Variables are not significant).

✅

Significance without P-Values

If the P-value column is missing, look at the 95% Confidence Interval. If both the Lower and Upper bounds are positive (e.g., [2.0, 4.0]), it does not contain zero.

Answer: The variable IS significant.

⚠️

The Adjusted R² Mathematical Impossibility

Exam Prompt: A model has an $R^2 = 0.80$. A new variable is added, making the new Adjusted $R^2 = 0.92$. What does this imply?

Adjusted $R^2$ applies a penalty fraction, meaning it can never exceed the raw $R^2$ of the same model ($Adj R^2 \le R^2$).

Answer: "None of the above." The provided data is mathematically impossible.

📊

Columns vs. Features

If a model has 2 explanatory variables ($k=2$), it has 2 features. But the dataset itself has 3 columns ($X_1, X_2,$ and the output $Y$).
