01

Core Concept & Terminology

The Demand Response (DR) curve mathematically visualizes the Law of Demand: for standard goods, quantity demanded is inversely proportional to the price. This inverse relationship dictates that as price increases, consumer willingness to purchase decreases.

Normal Goods

As income increases, demand increases.
Example: Upgrading from 4 ice creams a month to 2 ice creams a week as your purchasing power grows.

Inferior Goods

As income increases, demand decreases.
Example: Relying on cheap lemonade, but abandoning it for premium ice cream once your income allows.

Market Dynamics

Latent Demand (Hidden Demand)

Demand that has not materialized because the price exceeds the consumer's budget.
Example: A pizza costs ₹100, but you only have ₹60. The demand exists internally but is not actualized. As market price increases, latent demand increases.

Consumer Surplus

The extra utility a consumer enjoys when the market price is lower than their maximum willingness to pay.
Example: You are willing to pay ₹150 for a pizza, but it is on sale for ₹100. The ₹50 difference represents your consumer surplus.

Crucial Optimization Insight

By evaluating demand sensitivity via regression, businesses isolate exactly how a pricing adjustment dynamically shapes total revenue ($P \times Q$).

02

Mathematical Notation

$$ E = \left| \frac{\Delta Q / Q}{\Delta P / P} \right| = \left| \frac{dQ}{dP} \cdot \frac{P}{Q} \right| $$

Price Elasticity of Demand

Linear DR Curve & Derived Metrics

$$ D(p) = D_0 - mp $$

Market Size ($D_0$)

Demand when price is exactly zero ($P=0$). The absolute maximum possible consumer base.

Satiating Price ($P_{sat}$)

The price point where demand drops exactly to zero. Calculated as:
$P_{sat} = D_0 / m$

Linear Elasticity

It is not constant in a linear curve. It changes from $0$ to $\infty$ as price increases: $E = \frac{mP}{D_0 - mP}$

Non-Linear DR Curve

$$ D(p) = C \cdot p^{-\epsilon} $$

A constant elasticity curve where $\epsilon$ represents a static elasticity parameter mapping the exponential relationship directly.

04

Log-Log Transformation Walkthrough

1

Define the Non-Linear Base

Begin with the constant elasticity demand formula: $D = C \cdot P^{-\epsilon}$. Since this is exponential, standard linear regression cannot immediately process historical coordinates directly.

2

Deploy Logarithmic Transformation

Apply the natural logarithm ($\ln$) mapping symmetrically to both sides of the equation to mathematically unwrap the exponentiation: $$ \ln(D) = \ln(C \cdot P^{-\epsilon}) $$

3

Expand via Logarithmic Rules

Distribute the log. The multiplicative constant breaks out into an isolated addition, and the exponent ($\epsilon$) steps down to become a multiplier coefficient: $$ \ln(D) = \ln(C) - \epsilon \ln(P) $$

4

Align to Simple Linear Regression

The equation is now in strict $Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1X$ format, establishing:

  • $Y$ axis maps to $\ln(D)$
  • $X$ axis maps to $\ln(P)$
  • Intercept ($\beta_0$) maps to $\ln(C)$

Derived Extraction

$$ \text{Elasticity } (\epsilon) = -\beta_1 $$
05

Final Verdict & Application

Solved Optimization Problem

The Revenue Maximization Scenario (Chocolate)

A firm sells a chocolate bar for ₹5. At this price, 10 people purchase it.
Initial Revenue = ₹5 × 10 = ₹50
The firm wants to increase total revenue, so they raise the price to ₹6. Because demand is inversely proportional to price, the customer base drops to 9 people.
New Revenue = ₹6 × 9 = ₹54

💡

Key Insight:

Even though sales volume dropped, overall revenue ($P \times Q$) increased. This happened because the percentage drop in demand (10%) was smaller than the percentage increase in price (20%). This indicates the chocolate is acting as an inelastic good in this specific price window.

Elasticity Strategies

I

Inelastic Demand

$E < 1$

The product is largely essential (e.g., Medicine, Salt). Demand is structurally rigid. A 20% increase in price restricts demand by less than 20%.

Prescribed Action

Increase Price aggressively

To forcibly maximize revenue multiplier.

E

Elastic Demand

$E > 1$

Consumers operate with high price-sensitivity (luxury goods/high substitutes). A minor price increase triggers a massive drop in active demand volume.

Prescribed Action

Decrease Price strategically

To capture volume and maximize revenue.

Unit Elastic Condition

The percentage change in price mirrors the percentage change in quantity perfectly. The revenue structure is natively saturated and mathematically optimized.

$E = 1$
